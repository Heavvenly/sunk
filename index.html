Sunk

“He seems to feel his own worth, and the greatness of his fall” - Robert Walton


Yet he cannot choose but hear;
And thus spake on that ancient man,


PART I

At length did cross an Albatross,
As if a  soul,
round and round it flew
in mist or cloud, on mast or shroud,
The only object that I could distinguish
Was the bright moon

but my eyes were insensible to the
charms of nature

God save thee, ancient Mariner!

From the demoniacal corpse that plague thee thus!—
With my cross-bow —shot;

I saw the dull yellow eye of the creature open

PART II

And I had done a hellish thing,

and disgust filled my heart

I had killed the bird
that made the breeze to blow
I had killed the bird
That brought promise of new hopes
That brought food for discovery and wonder

All in a hot and copper sky,
The bloody Sun,

myself murderer of William, of Justine,  of Clerval
and lastly of my wife

Ah! well a-day! what evil looks
Had I from old and young!

I had begun life with benevolent intentions,
but how am I sunk

Instead of the cross, the daemon
About my neck was hung.

PART III & IV

They dropped down one by one.
And every soul, it passed me by,
they were dead, and I lived

Alone, alone, all, all alone,
Alone on a wide wide earth and sky
a thousand thousand slimy things
Lived on; and so did I

everlasting that curse,
And yet I could not die.

The charmèd fair lakes burnt alway
A still and awful red.

Beyond the stony beach of the sea,
I watched the glimmer of two eyes
They coiled and swam; and every track
Was a flash of golden desire

Their beauty could declare:
A spring of love gush  from my heart,
But I devote myself
in my life or death
to  destruction

O this world of misery!
I prayed for death
but revenge kept me alive

I dared to shake off   The Albatross
but the iron had eaten into my flesh
and sank into my miserable self

In Frankenstein, Mary Shelley uses The Rime of the Ancient Mariner to amplify the theme of accursedness after disrupting nature while contrasting how they learn from their mistakes to heighten Frankenstein’s tragedy.
